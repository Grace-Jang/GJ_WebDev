Frequently Asked Questions

What is hearing loss?

Hearing loss refers to a decrease or loss of the ability to hear and understand sounds. It can range from mild to severe and may affect one or both ears. The main types of hearing loss include conductive hearing loss, sensorineural hearing loss, and mixed hearing loss. Hearing loss can be caused by various factors, such as aging, noise exposure, genetic factors, and illnesses.

Is Surgery dangerous?

Cochlear implant surgery is generally considered a safe procedure. Like any surgery, it carries some risks, but the likelihood of serious complications is very low. Before the surgery, doctors thoroughly assess the patient's health and discuss any individual risk factors. Advances in medical technology and experienced surgeons have significantly improved the safety of the procedure. The recovery period varies for each individual, but most patients can quickly return to their daily activities.

How sounds are?

The experience of hearing sound for the first time through a cochlear implant varies from person to person. Initially, sounds may seem mechanical or robotic, and understanding speech can be challenging. However, as the brain adapts to these new auditory signals, the quality of sound improves over time. Most users experience significant improvements within weeks to months after activation. Many eventually develop the ability to understand conversations, enjoy music, and distinguish everyday environmental sounds.

Am I a candidate for a hearing implant?

Candidacy for a cochlear implant is determined by various factors. Generally, it is recommended for individuals with severe to profound sensorineural hearing loss who do not receive sufficient benefit from hearing aids. The evaluation process may include comprehensive hearing tests, medical assessments, imaging studies, and psychological evaluations. Factors such as age, duration of hearing loss, and language development are also considered. To determine if you are a candidate, it is important to consult with an audiologist or an otolaryngologist.
